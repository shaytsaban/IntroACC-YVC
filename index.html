Course Aims

The primary goal of financial accounting is to provide useful financial information for making sound economic decisions, particularly in the context of resource allocation. By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  • Understand the core principles and conceptual framework of financial accounting.
  • Record business transactions using journal entries and post them to ledger accounts.
  • Prepare and interpret the main financial statements: the Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet), Income Statement, and Statement of Changes in Equity.
  • Analyze financial data to assess a company's performance and financial health.
  • Address accounting issues related to key accounts like receivables, fixed assets, and inventory.

Lecture 1: Basics of Financial Accounting Values - 29.10.2025

This introductory lecture covers the five fundamental elements of accounting. Understanding these concepts is the first step to reading and preparing financial statements.

  • Assets: Economic resources controlled by the entity (e.g., cash, machinery, inventory) that are expected to provide future benefits.
  • Liabilities: The entity's obligations to other parties (e.g., loans, amounts owed to suppliers) that must be settled in the future.
  • Equity: The residual interest in the assets after deducting liabilities. It represents the ownership's stake in the company (Assets - Liabilities = Equity).
  • Income: Increases in economic benefits, such as revenues from sales or services, which result in an increase in equity.
  • Expenses: Decreases in economic benefits, such as the cost of goods sold or salaries, which result in a decrease in equity.

Lecture 2: Sales, Cost of Goods Sold, Gross Profit and basics of P&L - 5.11.2025

This lecture introduces key concepts of the Income Statement (Profit & Loss Statement) and how companies measure their profitability from operations.

  • Sales Revenue: The total income generated from selling goods or services to customers during a specific period.
  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): The direct costs attributable to producing the goods sold by a company, including materials and direct labor.
  • Gross Profit: The difference between sales revenue and cost of goods sold (Sales - COGS = Gross Profit), representing the profit before operating expenses.
  • Profit & Loss Statement (P&L): Also known as the Income Statement, it shows a company's revenues, expenses, and net income over a period of time.

Lecture 3: Journal Entries - 12.11.2025

Journal entries are the foundational mechanism for recording business transactions in accounting. They document the double-entry principle, ensuring that every transaction affects at least two accounts and maintains the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity). Properly recorded journal entries form the basis for all subsequent financial reporting and analysis.

Lecture 4: Income Recognition - 19.11.2025

Income recognition is a critical accounting principle that determines when revenue should be recorded. Under accrual accounting, revenue is recognized when it is earned, not necessarily when cash is received. This lecture explores the criteria for revenue recognition, including the transfer of control of goods or services to the customer, and how this principle impacts financial statements and cash flow analysis.

Lecture 6: Accounts Receivable and Provisions for Bad Debts - 3.12.2025

This lecture examines how companies manage and account for amounts owed by customers (accounts receivable). A key challenge in accounting for receivables is determining the carrying value when there is a risk that some customers will not pay. Under the expected credit loss model, companies must estimate and record provisions (allowances) for bad debts. This requires judgment about customer creditworthiness and historical collection patterns, and directly impacts both the balance sheet valuation of assets and the income statement through the recognition of bad debt expense. Understanding this topic is crucial for assessing a company's asset quality and profitability.
